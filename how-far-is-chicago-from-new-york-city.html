The Great Windy City vs. The Big Apple: A Tale of Two Coasts (and How Far Apart They Are)

Ever looked at a map of the USA and thought, "Dang, those two big cities sure seem far apart?" Well, my friend, you'd be right. But fear not, because today we're diving into the nitty-gritty of just how far apart Chicago and New York City actually are. Spoiler alert: it's not exactly a walk in the park (unless you're into some really long walks).

As the Eagle Flies (But Without Any Wings)

If you're picturing a tiny Chicago and a miniature NYC perched on a globe, with a determined eagle soaring between them, then you've got the right idea (minus the whole tiny city thing). The straight-line distance, also known as the as-the-crow-flies distance (because apparently eagles don't get all the credit), is roughly 712 miles (or 1,146 kilometers). That's a whole lotta flapping (or jet fuel) to cover.

But Wait, There's More! (Because Roads Aren't Straight)

Now, if you're planning a road trip from the land of deep-dish pizza to the city that never sleeps (or at least hits the snooze button a lot), you're in for a bit of a detour. Those pesky roads like to wind and turn, taking you on a scenic (or sometimes not-so-scenic) journey. Depending on your route, a car ride between these two metropolises can range from 788 to 800 miles (1,268 to 1,287 kilometers). Buckle up, buttercup, because that's a good 14-hour drive (give or take some traffic and snack breaks).

Pro Tip: Don't forget to pack your favorite travel playlist and a co-pilot who can tell terrible jokes to keep you awake!

So, How Far Is Chicago From New York City, Really?

Well, it depends! As we've seen, the distance can vary depending on whether you're a crow or a car. But hey, at least you now have a range to work with.

Here's the skinny:

  • As the crow flies: Roughly 712 miles (1,146 kilometers)
  • By car: Between 788 and 800 miles (1,268 to 1,287 kilometers)

Remember: Distance is just a number, and the adventure of getting from Point A to Point B is half the fun (well, maybe a quarter of the fun, depending on how you feel about traffic jams).
